### Rose

**Names:** Роза / Rose / *Rosa* spp.; in commercial cut flowers often hybrid garden roses and greenhouse florist roses.

**Seasonality:** Locally garden roses peak late spring through summer. Commercial cut roses are effectively year-round through greenhouse production and imports. For cards, mark as "year-round commercial base" with stronger local garden character in spring-summer.

**Composition Role:** focal, classic mass, romantic accent, mono-bouquet base, luxury commercial stem. Spray roses also work as small accent/filler; garden roses work as lush focal texture.

**Character:** The most culturally legible flower in floristry: formal, romantic, ceremonial, sometimes severe. The exact mood changes by group: hybrid tea reads clean and architectural; spray rose reads tender and detailed; garden/English rose reads fragrant, nostalgic, painterly.

**Freshness Checks:**

- Head should feel firm and hydrated, not papery or collapsing at the neck.
- Outer guard petals may be marked; this is not always damage, but slimy, transparent, or heavily browned petals suggest age or cold/handling stress.
- Stem should be firm, leaves crisp, and the neck under the head should not bend.
- Avoid roses with grey mold, blackened petal edges, mushy stem ends, or sour bucket smell.
- Choose stage by use: tighter for transport and later opening, more open for immediate event work.

**Processing After Purchase:**

- Remove packaging and inspect for broken heads, mold, and leaves below future water line.
- Strip all foliage and thorns that will sit below water.
- Recut 1-2 cm from the stem at about 45 degrees with a clean sharp knife or secateurs.
- Hydrate in clean water with commercial flower food; keep bunches upright and uncrowded.
- Remove damaged guard petals only after hydration when the head is less vulnerable.

**Storage And Vase Life:** Typical florist expectation is about 5-10 days, but variety, transport, hydration, and flower food change performance. Store cool, upright, away from ethylene sources such as ripening fruit. Keep buckets and vases very clean; bacteria shortens vase life and blocks uptake.

**Beginner Mistakes:**

- Leaving leaves in water and creating bacterial water quickly.
- Removing too many guard petals and bruising the rose before it has rehydrated.
- Buying very open heads for a job that needs travel or next-day installation.
- Mixing old and new stems in the same dirty bucket.

**Three Useful Varieties / Types / Groups:**

1. Hybrid tea / large-headed commercial rose — the classic long-stem, one-head-per-stem florist rose; useful for formal bouquets, mono work, and clear focal points. Examples to research later: Red Naomi, Mondial, Quicksand, Playa Blanca.
2. Spray rose — branched stems with multiple smaller heads; useful for scale, detail, boutonnières, small arrangements, and romantic filler. Examples to research later: Bombastic, Babe, Mirabel.
3. Garden / English / garden-style rose — cupped, many-petalled, often fragrant and expensive; useful for editorial, wedding, garden-style work. Examples to research later: Juliet, Patience, Keira.

**All Other Varieties:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden_roses — broad rose class overview. For final named cultivar cards, use breeder/grower catalogs in addition to Wikipedia.

**Card Labels:** year-round / focal / medium-sensitive / very high type complexity

**Card-Ready Notes:**

- poster card: "Роза — базовый язык флористики: от строгой классики до садовой мягкости."
- story card: "У розы не один характер, а целая система характеров: крупная одиночная головка, мелкая спрей-ветка, ароматная садовая чашка."
- care card: "45-degree recut, clean water, no foliage below water, cool storage, remove guard petals carefully."
- varieties card: "Start with three florist groups: hybrid tea, spray, garden-style. Named cultivars are the next layer."

**Sources:** RHS Roses genus guide; RHS modern bush rose guide; FloraLife rose vase-life study; Garden roses Wikipedia index.

### Peony

**Names:** Пион / Peony / *Paeonia* spp.; common cut-flower peonies are often herbaceous peony cultivars, especially *Paeonia lactiflora* and hybrids.

**Seasonality:** Strongly seasonal. Local field peonies peak late spring to early summer. Import and stored peonies can extend availability, but the flower still reads as a seasonal luxury. In card logic: "spring-early summer hero."

**Composition Role:** lush focal, soft mass, romantic seasonal statement, luxury wedding/editorial flower. Works best when allowed space to open; not ideal for tight, hard-edged designs where every bloom must stay the same size.

**Character:** Soft abundance, transition, celebration, garden luxury. Peony changes dramatically from small round bud to huge open flower, so it teaches timing better than almost any spring flower.

**Freshness Checks:**

- Bud should show color and feel like a soft marshmallow for reliable opening; rock-hard green buds may fail to open.
- Avoid fully blown flowers if the bouquet must travel or last several days.
- Check for sticky residue, bruised petals, botrytis, or blackened outer petals.
- Stems should be firm and leaves healthy; limp leaves can indicate dehydration.
- Buy by event timing: tight colored buds for later, softer/opening stems for immediate visual impact.

**Processing After Purchase:**

- Remove lower foliage to reduce water loss and keep water clean.
- Recut stems cleanly at an angle.
- Hydrate in deep clean water in a cool place.
- Allow time for opening; do not force by placing in hot/sunny conditions unless the job requires accelerated bloom and risk is acceptable.
- If storing, keep cool and monitor stage daily; peonies can open quickly once warm.

**Storage And Vase Life:** Often 5-10 days depending on harvest stage and room temperature. The "marshmallow stage" is key: it gives both opening reliability and better vase performance. Cool storage slows opening; warmth accelerates it. Once fully open, the peak decorative stage is shorter.

**Beginner Mistakes:**

- Buying hard green buds and expecting them to open for tomorrow's event.
- Buying fully open blooms too early for a wedding/install.
- Leaving too much foliage, causing water loss and crowding.
- Forgetting that one stem can double or triple visually as it opens.

**Three Useful Varieties / Types / Groups:**

1. Herbaceous peony — the main cut-flower workhorse; dies back in winter; many classic double and semi-double forms.
2. Tree peony — woody stems, huge dramatic blooms, more garden/collector logic than everyday cut-flower base.
3. Itoh / intersectional peony — hybrid between herbaceous and tree peonies; often strong stems and unusual colors; useful as a premium knowledge layer.

**All Other Varieties:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peony — broad species/cultivar overview. For named cultivar work, cross-check with American Peony Society or grower catalogs.

**Card Labels:** late spring-early summer / focal / timing-sensitive / high type complexity

**Card-Ready Notes:**

- poster card: "Пион — цветок ожидания: маленький бутон превращается в большое садовое облако."
- story card: "Главный урок пиона — стадия. Он красив не только цветком, но и моментом раскрытия."
- care card: "Buy at colored soft-bud stage, strip foliage, hydrate cool, plan opening time."
- varieties card: "Begin with herbaceous, tree, and Itoh groups before going into named cultivars."

**Sources:** RHS herbaceous/intersectional peony guide; RHS intersectional peony guide; NC State Paeonia herbaceous profile; Commercial Specialty Cut Flower Production: Peonies; Peony Wikipedia index.

### Moluccella

**Names:** Молюцелла / Bells of Ireland, shellflower / *Moluccella laevis*.

**Seasonality:** Summer into early autumn in field production; commercially used as a fresh or dried green structural material. In contemporary floristry it can read as spring-green, architectural, and modern even when used outside strict garden season.

**Composition Role:** line, structure, vertical accent, green texture, architectural filler. It is not a soft filler like gypsophila; it gives direction and height.

**Character:** Graphic, fresh, slightly odd, botanical. Its green bell-shaped calyces make it feel designed already, almost like a plant-object rather than a flower.

**Freshness Checks:**

- Bells/calyces should be green, firm, and evenly stacked on the spike.
- Avoid stems where lower bells are brown, collapsed, or mushy.
- Check for sharp tiny thorns on stems; handle with care.
- Stems should be straight enough for design use unless a curved botanical line is desired.
- For dried use, bells should be firm, not watery or bruised.

**Processing After Purchase:**

- Recut stems and place in clean water.
- Remove any damaged lower bells or leaves that sit below water.
- Give space in the bucket; spikes can tangle or bruise.
- Handle stems carefully because tiny thorns can scratch hands.
- For drying, hang or place upright when bells are mature and firm.

**Storage And Vase Life:** Practical vase-life expectation is often about 7-10 days. Store cool in clean water. It can also be dried, which gives it a second use as a structural material. Avoid crushing the bells; bruised green tissue quickly looks tired.

**Beginner Mistakes:**

- Treating it as a soft filler instead of a line/architecture material.
- Using too many stems and making a bouquet stiff or spiky.
- Ignoring the tiny thorns during processing.
- Buying immature soft stems for dried work.

**Three Useful Varieties / Types / Groups:**

1. *Moluccella laevis* / Bells of Ireland — the main florist material; bright green bell spikes.
2. Fresh green spike market form — use when calyces are green, hydrated, and architectural; best for modern vertical designs.
3. Dried moluccella / mature-firm stage — useful when bells have firmed and can be dried for lasting structure.

**All Other Varieties:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moluccella_laevis — species overview. Named cultivar diversity is limited in common floristry; track stem length, maturity, and fresh/dried use more than cultivar names.

**Card Labels:** summer-autumn / line-structure / low-medium sensitivity / low cultivar complexity

**Card-Ready Notes:**

- poster card: "Молюцелла — зеленая вертикаль, которая сразу строит композицию."
- story card: "Название Bells of Ireland обманчиво: растение не ирландское, но стало символом удачи и зеленого архитектурного жеста."
- care card: "Recut, clean water, remove lower material, handle thorny stems carefully."
- varieties card: "For this flower, variety logic is maturity/use logic: fresh spike, tall market stem, dried structural stem."

**Sources:** NC State Moluccella profile; Britannica Bells of Ireland; Johnny's Bells of Ireland; Floret Bells of Ireland library.

### Hydrangea

**Names:** Гортензия / Hydrangea / *Hydrangea* spp.; common florist material often includes *Hydrangea macrophylla*, with panicle and smooth hydrangeas as important type knowledge.

**Seasonality:** Summer garden flower with import availability across wider periods. Antique/late-stage hydrangeas are a separate aesthetic and handling category. For cards: "summer volume; import and antique stages extend the story."

**Composition Role:** mass, volume, soft architecture, base cloud, color field. It can replace many small flowers with one large head but needs water discipline.

**Character:** A water-sensitive volume flower. It looks soft and generous, but behaves like a technical material: hydration, maturity, and handling determine whether it looks luxurious or collapses.

**Freshness Checks:**

- Head should feel hydrated and springy, not limp or papery.
- Decorative florets should be developed; immature heads can wilt faster and fail to hold.
- Leaves should be crisp, but many leaves should be removed for design to reduce water loss.
- Avoid stems with collapsed florets, browning edges, mushy stems, or dry papery heads unless intentionally buying antique/dried material.
- Check stem thickness and woodiness; very woody stems may need extra hydration attention.

**Processing After Purchase:**

- Recut at about 45 degrees; with woody stems, some florists lightly split the base to improve uptake.
- Remove most foliage, especially anything below water and excess large leaves.
- Hydrate deeply in clean water; avoid leaving heads out on the worktable.
- If wilted, rehydrate promptly; full-head soaking or other revival techniques should be treated as florist techniques, not magic guarantees.
- Use clean buckets and avoid warm dry air.

**Storage And Vase Life:** Practical expectation often about 5-10 days, but postharvest treatment, maturity, cultivar, and storage duration matter strongly. Hydrangea is very sensitive to poor care and water stress. Cooler humid conditions and clean water improve performance.

**Beginner Mistakes:**

- Designing with dehydrated stems without restoring turgor.
- Leaving too much foliage and accelerating water loss.
- Using immature heads that cannot hold well.
- Assuming all hydrangeas behave like classic blue mophead macrophylla.

**Three Useful Varieties / Types / Groups:**

1. *Hydrangea macrophylla* — classic bigleaf/mophead/lacecap florist reference; round heads, strong color associations, hydration-sensitive.
2. *Hydrangea paniculata* — cone-shaped panicle form; reads more structural and seasonal, often white-green-pink aging.
3. *Hydrangea arborescens* — smooth hydrangea, Annabelle-type round forms; useful for understanding white/green garden volume and different stem/head behavior.

**All Other Varieties:** https://www.rhs.org.uk/plants/hydrangea/ — RHS hydrangea plant guide and linked cultivar pages. Also use https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrangea for broad species orientation.

**Card Labels:** summer-import / mass-volume / high sensitivity / high type complexity

**Card-Ready Notes:**

- poster card: "Гортензия — облако воды: роскошный объем, который требует дисциплины."
- story card: "Ее красота держится на тургоре. Это не просто большой цветок, а тест на аккуратность флориста."
- care card: "Mature head, deep hydration, remove foliage, cool humid storage, restore turgor before design."
- varieties card: "Teach types before cultivar names: macrophylla, paniculata, arborescens."

**Sources:** RHS hydrangea pruning guide; NC State hydrangeas overview; Hort Journal hydrangea vase-life stage research; Floret cut and care guide; existing `research/hydrangea-structure-notes.md`.

### Ranunculus

**Names:** Ранункулюс / Ranunculus, Persian buttercup / *Ranunculus asiaticus* for the main florist type.

**Seasonality:** Spring and early-season luxury flower. Greenhouse and import production can extend availability, but it visually belongs to cool-season spring work.

**Composition Role:** focal-small, detail focal, romantic texture, garden-style accent, delicate mass in clusters. Butterfly types can act as airy movement; standard double types read denser and rose-like.

**Character:** Layered, fragile, luminous. Ranunculus gives a bouquet delicacy and complexity without the heaviness of a rose or peony. It is beautiful partly because it looks breakable.

**Freshness Checks:**

- Stems should be firm but are naturally delicate; avoid crushed or bent necks.
- Heads should be colored and starting to open; overly tight buds may stay small, while over-open blooms shed faster.
- Petal edges should not be browning or transparent.
- Leaves and stems should not be slimy.
- Check bunches for tangled heads; ranunculus bruises and snaps easily.

**Processing After Purchase:**

- Handle gently and keep upright; stems snap easily.
- Recut stems and place into clean water or a low-sugar holding solution where available.
- Remove foliage below water.
- Sleeve or support bunches to prevent flower heads and stems tangling.
- Do not overcrowd buckets.

**Storage And Vase Life:** Grower standards often place ranunculus around 10-14 days under good postharvest conditions. For general florist use, expect roughly 7-10+ days depending on stage, type, and handling. Store cool but avoid damaging cold; protect fragile stems in transport.

**Beginner Mistakes:**

- Treating stems like roses and snapping them during processing.
- Buying over-open flowers for a job that needs travel.
- Overcrowding buckets so heads tangle and bruise.
- Ignoring type differences: butterfly ranunculus behaves visually very differently from dense double types.

**Three Useful Varieties / Types / Groups:**

1. Butterfly ranunculus — lighter, airy, often glossy petals; excellent for movement and delicate editorial work.
2. Cloni / Italian ranunculus — premium large-headed commercial group; useful for luxury focal detail and strong flower form.
3. Tecolote / standard florist ranunculus — classic double, many-petalled type; useful for spring bouquets and approachable floral texture.

**All Other Varieties:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ranunculus_asiaticus and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Ranunculus_species — broad orientation. For commercial series, verify with grower/supplier catalogs.

**Card Labels:** spring / detail focal / medium-high sensitivity / medium-high type complexity

**Card-Ready Notes:**

- poster card: "Ранункулюс — маленькая архитектура лепестков, весенний люкс без тяжести."
- story card: "Он похож на розу, но ведет себя нежнее: не центральная власть, а мерцающий слой."
- care card: "Handle gently, recut, clean water/holding solution, keep upright, avoid tangling."
- varieties card: "Show three market types: butterfly, Cloni/Italian, Tecolote/standard."

**Sources:** RHS ranunculus growing guide; Kew POWO *Ranunculus asiaticus*; Johnny's floral standards; Johnny's ranunculus tech sheet; Ranunculus asiaticus Wikipedia index.
