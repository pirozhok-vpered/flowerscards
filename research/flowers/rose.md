# Rose

**Names:** Роза / Rose / *Rosa* spp.; in commercial cut flowers often hybrid garden roses and greenhouse florist roses.

**Seasonality:** Locally garden roses peak late spring through summer. Commercial cut roses are effectively year-round through greenhouse production and imports. For cards, mark as "year-round commercial base" with stronger local garden character in spring-summer.

**Composition Role:** focal, classic mass, romantic accent, mono-bouquet base, luxury commercial stem. Spray roses also work as small accent/filler; garden roses work as lush focal texture.

**Character:** The most culturally legible flower in floristry: formal, romantic, ceremonial, sometimes severe. The exact mood changes by group: hybrid tea reads clean and architectural; spray rose reads tender and detailed; garden/English rose reads fragrant, nostalgic, painterly.

**Freshness Checks:**

- Head should feel firm and hydrated, not papery or collapsing at the neck.
- Outer guard petals may be marked; this is not always damage, but slimy, transparent, or heavily browned petals suggest age or cold/handling stress.
- Stem should be firm, leaves crisp, and the neck under the head should not bend.
- Avoid roses with grey mold, blackened petal edges, mushy stem ends, or sour bucket smell.
- Choose stage by use: tighter for transport and later opening, more open for immediate event work.

**Processing After Purchase:**

- Remove packaging and inspect for broken heads, mold, and leaves below future water line.
- Strip all foliage and thorns that will sit below water.
- Recut 1-2 cm from the stem at about 45 degrees with a clean sharp knife or secateurs.
- Hydrate in clean water with commercial flower food; keep bunches upright and uncrowded.
- Remove damaged guard petals only after hydration when the head is less vulnerable.

**Storage And Vase Life:** Typical florist expectation is about 5-10 days, but variety, transport, hydration, and flower food change performance. Store cool, upright, away from ethylene sources such as ripening fruit. Keep buckets and vases very clean; bacteria shortens vase life and blocks uptake.

**Beginner Mistakes:**

- Leaving leaves in water and creating bacterial water quickly.
- Removing too many guard petals and bruising the rose before it has rehydrated.
- Buying very open heads for a job that needs travel or next-day installation.
- Mixing old and new stems in the same dirty bucket.

**Three Useful Varieties / Types / Groups:**

1. Hybrid tea / large-headed commercial rose — the classic long-stem, one-head-per-stem florist rose; useful for formal bouquets, mono work, and clear focal points. Examples to research later: Red Naomi, Mondial, Quicksand, Playa Blanca.
2. Spray rose — branched stems with multiple smaller heads; useful for scale, detail, boutonnières, small arrangements, and romantic filler. Examples to research later: Bombastic, Babe, Mirabel.
3. Garden / English / garden-style rose — cupped, many-petalled, often fragrant and expensive; useful for editorial, wedding, garden-style work. Examples to research later: Juliet, Patience, Keira.

**All Other Varieties:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden_roses — broad rose class overview. For final named cultivar cards, use breeder/grower catalogs in addition to Wikipedia.

**Card Labels:** year-round / focal / medium-sensitive / very high type complexity

**Card-Ready Notes:**

- poster card: "Роза — базовый язык флористики: от строгой классики до садовой мягкости."
- story card: "У розы не один характер, а целая система характеров: крупная одиночная головка, мелкая спрей-ветка, ароматная садовая чашка."
- care card: "45-degree recut, clean water, no foliage below water, cool storage, remove guard petals carefully."
- varieties card: "Start with three florist groups: hybrid tea, spray, garden-style. Named cultivars are the next layer."
