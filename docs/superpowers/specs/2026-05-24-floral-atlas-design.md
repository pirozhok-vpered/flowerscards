# Floral Atlas Design

Date: 2026-05-24

## Purpose

Create a visually strong and practically useful floral atlas for a beginning florist. The atlas should feel like a designed product, not a dry reference document: minimal, editorial, atmospheric, and pleasant to revisit while still being useful for real work with flowers.

The first version will focus on a basic florist assortment and will be built so the assortment can expand later without changing the whole system.

## Core Outcome

The project will produce a hybrid learning system:

1. A 16:9 presentation for visual learning and browsing.
2. Practical card-style pages that can become a PDF, Notion-style reference, print cards, or a future Figma product.

The first version should establish the visual language and content system before scaling to every flower. The recommended first prototype set is rose, tulip, and hydrangea.

## Audience

The primary user is a beginner florist who wants to learn flowers from the perspective of someone who works with them directly: seasonality, varieties, care, storage, vase life, composition, sourcing regions, and practical mistakes.

The atlas should support both study and inspiration. It should be beautiful enough to feel collectible and clear enough to use as a working prompt before buying, processing, or composing flowers.

## Visual Direction

The chosen direction is archival botanical poster plus designed flower index.

References from the user point toward:

- one flower as the central subject;
- flat, saturated, slightly muted backgrounds;
- poster-like composition with small typographic details around the page;
- visible grain, scan, paper, or print texture;
- contrast between small functional grotesque type and expressive serif or italic display type;
- a botanical object rather than a commercial bouquet;
- atmosphere without losing legibility.

The design should not copy any reference. It should translate the mood into an original system for an educational florist atlas.

## Visual System

The atlas uses two connected card types for each flower.

### Poster Card

The Poster Card is the emotional and visual entry point.

It contains:

- one main flower image or isolated stem;
- a seasonal or role-based background color;
- large flower name;
- Latin name as a secondary layer;
- small poster typography with date codes, season codes, role labels, or sourcing hints;
- light grain or print texture;
- generous negative space.

The goal is to make each flower feel like a collectible botanical poster.

### Index Card

The Index Card is the practical learning side.

It uses the same palette and typographic language but prioritizes readability.

It contains:

- common name;
- Latin name;
- seasonality;
- approximate vase life;
- care and processing notes;
- storage notes;
- varieties or common market types;
- growing and sourcing regions;
- role in composition;
- useful pairings;
- beginner mistakes;
- source notes.

The Index Card should feel designed, not like a spreadsheet. Data can be organized with small labels, scales, badges, thin rules, and compact editorial blocks.

## Palette Logic

The palette should vary across cards while staying part of one family.

Seasonal palette groups:

- Winter: graphite, cold milk, silver green, burgundy.
- Spring: sage, cream, pale yellow, dusty pink.
- Summer: tomato red, grass green, cream, cornflower accent.
- Autumn: olive, ochre, wine, warm gray.

Palette choices can also reflect flower temperature and composition role:

- focal flowers may receive stronger backgrounds;
- airy or filler flowers may use lighter, quieter grounds;
- greenery and structural materials may lean into olive, graphite, and muted green.

## Typography

Typography should feel modern, editorial, and precise.

Recommended hierarchy:

- large serif or high-contrast display type for the main flower name;
- clean grotesque for facts, labels, and small navigation text;
- Latin names may use italic or serif styling;
- microtype can create the poster/product feeling but must not carry essential information.

Russian is the primary language. Latin and English names are supporting layers.

## Photo Strategy

The first version uses free photo sources only.

Photo selection must be art-directed:

- avoid random stock bouquets;
- prefer isolated stems, single flowers, botanical silhouettes, close details, and clean backgrounds;
- favor natural or controlled light;
- avoid overly wedding-like, decorative, or commercial flower shop imagery;
- keep color temperature and texture consistent across the set;
- do not use a photo of a similar flower as proof of a precise cultivar.

Every selected image should record:

- source platform;
- author or photographer;
- direct image/page link;
- license page;
- notes about suitability.

Free photo sources may include Unsplash, Pexels, Pixabay, Wikimedia Commons where suitable, and other sources with clear reusable licensing. Paid stock, Figma, and custom commissioned design are out of scope for this first version.

## Fact Strategy

Facts and visuals are separate layers.

Factual content should be sourced from:

- professional florist care guides;
- flower farms and grower documentation;
- breeder and supplier pages for varieties;
- wholesale flower references;
- botanical references for Latin names and classification;
- multiple sources for variable claims such as seasonality and vase life.

Regional variation must be explicit. Seasonality and availability can differ across local production, Dutch auctions, Ecuador, Kenya, Italy, France, and other supply regions.

Use practical wording:

- "usually";
- "often";
- "typical vase life";
- "peak season";
- "available through import";
- "depends on supplier and cold chain."

Avoid false precision when the market reality is variable.

## Starting Assortment

The starting flower set:

- rose;
- tulip;
- peony;
- hydrangea;
- ranunculus;
- chrysanthemum;
- lisianthus;
- alstroemeria;
- carnation;
- greenery and fillers.

Greenery and fillers should be handled as a grouped section in the first version rather than a full Poster Card for every material.

Possible greenery and filler examples:

- eucalyptus;
- ruscus;
- asparagus fern;
- salal;
- gypsophila;
- statice;
- waxflower;
- matthiola where useful as a fragrant or supporting material.

## Presentation Structure

The first full atlas should contain approximately 30-35 slides.

Suggested structure:

1. Cover.
2. How to read the atlas.
3. Core florist concepts.
4. Flower roles in composition.
5. Seasonality and sourcing.
6. Vase life, freshness, and cold chain.
7. Basic stem processing.
8. Color, form, and texture.
9. Common beginner mistakes.
10. Flower cards: Poster Card plus Index Card for each starting flower.
11. Greenery and fillers section.
12. Summary cheat sheets.

## Practical Cheat Sheets

The atlas should include compact reference pages:

- seasonality calendar;
- vase life table;
- processing and care table;
- flower role matrix;
- common beginner mistakes;
- pairings by form, texture, and color mood.

These pages must keep the same design language and should not become plain office tables.

## First Prototype Scope

The first production pass should create a strong prototype before scaling.

Prototype set:

- rose;
- tulip;
- hydrangea.

For each prototype flower:

- one Poster Card;
- one Index Card;
- researched facts with sources;
- selected free image candidates;
- notes on whether the visual/factual material is strong enough to scale.

After the prototype is approved, the system can expand to the rest of the starting assortment.

## Success Criteria

The first version succeeds if:

- the design feels closer to an art-directed botanical product than to a generic educational deck;
- the cards are pleasant to look at and useful to read;
- the visual system can expand to more flowers;
- practical facts are sourced and phrased responsibly;
- free images feel curated and stylistically coherent;
- seasonal and regional variability is handled honestly;
- the user can use the atlas as a study aid and as inspiration for future florist work.

## Out Of Scope For First Version

- Figma implementation.
- Paid stock purchasing.
- Claims of exact cultivar identification from generic images.
- A complete encyclopedia of all florist flowers.
- A commercial-ready product launch package.
- Custom photo shoot.

## Next Step After Design Approval

After this design is approved, the next step is to write an implementation plan for:

1. research workflow;
2. source tracking;
3. photo search and selection;
4. prototype card creation;
5. presentation assembly;
6. verification and review.
